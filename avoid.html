Places to Avoid

Lawson Heights - Pennsylvania

This area has seen a number of Bigfoot sightings, particularly in the more wooded regions. The dense foliage provides ample cover for the elusive creature.

Adirondack Mountains - New York

The Adirondacks are known for their vast wilderness and have been the site of numerous Bigfoot reports. Hikers and campers should exercise caution when exploring this region.

Pisgah National Forest - North Carolina

This extensive forest is known for its diverse wildlife and has been the site of numerous Bigfoot sightings. Hikers should exercise caution and be aware of their surroundings.

Woodland Park - Colorado

The dense forests and rugged terrain of Woodland Park have made it a hotspot for Bigfoot activity. Visitors should remain vigilant while exploring this area.

Shenandoah National Park - Virginia

This park's vast wilderness areas have been the site of several Bigfoot sightings. Hikers and campers are advised to stay on marked trails and be aware of their surroundings.

Olympic National Park - Washington

The diverse ecosystems of Olympic National Park, from temperate rainforests to rugged coastlines, have been linked to numerous Bigfoot reports. Visitors should take precautions when venturing into remote areas.

General tips to avoid

Your Final Steps

First, make plenty of noise while hiking—talk loudly, play music, or use a whistle—since most wildlife, including Bigfoot if it exists, tends to avoid humans. Avoid traveling alone and stick to well-marked trails whenever possible. Carry bright lights or flares to discourage anything from approaching your campsite at night.

Keep food sealed and stored away from your tent to prevent attracting curious creatures. If you hear strange noises or see movement in the distance, don’t investigate—slowly back away and leave the area calmly. Trust your instincts, stay alert, and remember that it’s always better to be cautious than curious.

Remember to respect wildlife and the environment during your expedition.
